Legal & Regulatory Landscape

CFTC & Prediction Markets (Event Contracts)

The regulatory environment for prediction markets is evolving rapidly. Here are the key facts:

KalshiEx LLC v. CFTC (2024-2025) — Landmark Case

  • In September 2024, a DC District Court ruled in Kalshi’s favor, finding that the CFTC exceeded its statutory authority in blocking political event contracts
  • The court held that Kalshi’s political events contracts did not involve “unlawful activity” or “gaming” under the CEA
  • The DC Circuit denied the CFTC’s emergency stay request
  • In May 2025, the CFTC dropped its appeal after the change in administration
  • Result: Political event contracts on CFTC-registered exchanges are now permissible

2026 Regulatory Shift

  • January 2026: CFTC Chair Michael Selig announced prediction markets rulemaking
  • February 2026: CFTC withdrew prior proposed rule that would have prohibited political and sports event contracts; filed amicus brief asserting “exclusive jurisdiction” over event contracts (vs. state regulation)
  • March 2026: CFTC issued Staff Letter No. 26-08 detailing views on event contract regulation; published ANPRM requesting comment on broad event contract regulation issues
  • Market growth: Event contract listings surged from ~5/year (2006-2020) to ~1,600 in 2025
  • DCM applications have more than doubled, with most from prediction market operators
Source: Sidley Austin • Federal Register ANPRM • Greenberg Traurig • Brownstein (Kalshi v. CFTC)

CFTC Insider Trading & Enforcement

  • February 2026: CFTC issued advisory addressing insider trading on prediction markets, following two Kalshi enforcement cases
  • One case involved a political candidate trading on his own candidacy (May 2025)
  • In March 2026, Kalshi imposed bans on political candidates betting on their own markets and restricted sports participants from related event trading
  • Rep. Ritchie Torres introduced the Public Integrity in Financial Prediction Markets Act of 2026 to ban federal employees from trading on outcomes they might influence
Interview relevance: As GC, you would need to build internal policies preventing insider trading on Plaee’s platform — particularly for any B2C markets. This is a cutting-edge compliance challenge.

Israeli Military Insider Trading Scandal (February 2026)

  • An Israeli Air Force major and a civilian were indicted for using classified military intelligence to bet on Polymarket regarding the timing of IDF operations
  • One interrogated individual claimed “the entire squadron is on Polymarket, the entire air force is betting”
  • This is the first known case of military secrets being used for prediction market bets
  • Congressional probe launched — Rep. James Comer seeking information from Kalshi and Polymarket CEOs
  • Over 80 Polymarket users flagged for suspicious trading patterns
Interview relevance: This Israeli military case will likely come up in conversation given that Plaee operates from Israel. It underscores the critical importance of robust market integrity and surveillance systems for any prediction market platform.
Source: CFTC Press Release • Lowenstein Sandler • NPR • Times of Israel

Sweepstakes Casino Legal Framework (US)

Plaee’s sweepstakes business operates in an increasingly challenged legal environment:

How Sweepstakes Casinos Work

  • Players use Gold Coins (GC) for casual play (purchased, no cash value) and Sweepstakes Coins (SC) for prize-eligible play
  • SC can be obtained free (mail-in, bonus with GC purchase) — this removes the “consideration” element from the gambling definition
  • Legal argument: Not gambling because no purchase is necessary to enter the sweepstakes

2025-2026 State Bans — Critical Threat

  • 13 US states now ban sweepstakes casinos (as of April 2026), including: California, New York, Connecticut, Montana, New Jersey, Nevada, Indiana, Maine, and others
  • California’s ban (AB 831, effective Jan 1, 2026) had outsized impact — accounted for 17-20% of total US sweepstakes revenue
  • Montana (SB 555) imposes penalties up to $50K fines and 10 years in prison; New Jersey imposes $25K-$250K civil penalties per violation
  • Florida’s HB 591 is moving through committee in 2026 with felony-level penalties
  • Tennessee AG issued cease-and-desist to ~40 operators (Dec 2025); SB 2136 passed Senate 32-0 (March 2026)
  • 100+ class action lawsuits filed against sweepstakes casinos in 2025
  • Market revenue revised downward: Eilers & Krejcik forecast $5.6B for 2026 (down from $7.7B projection), a 10% contraction
Strategic implication: The sweepstakes model is under pressure. Plaee’s GC will need to navigate a patchwork of state bans, advise on which markets remain viable, and potentially develop alternative legal frameworks.
Source: GI Blog • iGaming Business • KPMG Sweepstakes Primer

DeFi Prediction Markets & Regulatory Challenges

Plaee’s global track uses self-custodial DeFi architecture — this creates unique legal questions:

  • Polymarket: Relaunched in US (Dec 2025) after acquiring QCEX for $112M (CFTC license). But faces state-level opposition: Nevada enforcement action, Tennessee cease-and-desist, Massachusetts preliminary injunction
  • International bans: Polymarket banned/blocked in 33+ countries including Portugal, Hungary, Brazil, Indonesia
  • Third Circuit ruling (April 2026): Strengthened prediction markets by holding that CFTC has jurisdiction over sports event contracts, pre-empting state gambling laws
  • Self-custodial implications: When users control their own keys (inabit’s model), the platform may argue it doesn’t take custody of funds, potentially limiting regulatory exposure
Source: Gambling Insider • Polymarket Wikipedia

State vs. Federal Jurisdiction Battle

A critical legal battleground for Plaee’s US operations:

  • CFTC asserts exclusive federal jurisdiction over event contracts traded on derivatives exchanges
  • Multiple states (Nevada, Massachusetts, New Jersey, Tennessee) are pushing back, arguing prediction markets are gambling subject to state law
  • Third Circuit (April 2026): Ruled that CEA pre-empts New Jersey gambling laws for sports event contracts on DCMs
  • But Massachusetts (Jan 2026): Issued preliminary injunction against Kalshi, creating a circuit split
  • Arizona (March 2026): Filed 20-count criminal information against Kalshi for operating “illegal gambling” without a state license (halted by federal TRO April 10)
  • April 2026: CFTC sued Connecticut and Arizona; DOJ sued Illinois, Connecticut, and Arizona — seeking declaratory judgments that the CEA pre-empts state gambling laws
  • As of early 2026: 19+ federal lawsuits challenging prediction market platform legality; 39 state AGs signed amicus brief supporting state enforcement
GC perspective: This federal-vs-state tension is exactly where Plaee’s GC will spend significant time. Understanding both the CFTC regulatory framework and state-by-state gambling law is essential.
Source: Stinson LLP • Mondaq

Israel Gambling Law

Since Plaee has operations in Israel (Ramat Gan / Bnei Brak) with corporate presence in Cyprus:

Legal Framework

  • Israeli Penal Law 5737 (1977) prohibits almost all forms of gambling, with only two exceptions: the National Lottery (Mifal HaPais) and the state-owned Israel Sports Betting Board (ISBB)
  • Online gambling, online casinos, and poker are all prohibited. No private or foreign operator can obtain a gambling license — all legal gambling is state-monopolized
  • Players face fines up to 29,200 ILS (~$8,000) or one year imprisonment for unlawful gambling
  • ISP blocking has been in effect since late 2018; police conduct raids and asset forfeiture
  • Despite enforcement, an estimated $3.5 billion in illegal gambling occurs annually with 150+ online platforms serving Hebrew/English speakers

Israel as a Gaming Tech Hub (The Paradox)

  • Israel is one of the world’s major hubs for gambling technology companies while prohibiting gambling domestically
  • Key examples: 888 Holdings (founded 1997 in Israel, registered in BVI/Gibraltar), Playtech (founded 1999 by Teddy Sagi in Tel Aviv, registered in Estonia)
  • These companies maintain R&D in Israel but are forbidden from serving Israeli residents
  • Israel is also a social casino powerhouse — social casino games (no real money wagered) fall outside the Penal Law and are legal to develop from Israel
  • Plaee’s April 2026 press release lists its contact address as Limassol, Cyprus — following the standard Israeli gaming tech structure
Interview note: Be prepared to discuss how to structure multi-entity corporate architectures that separate technology development (Israel) from regulated gaming operations (Cyprus/other jurisdictions). This is standard practice for Israeli gaming companies — not regulatory arbitrage but practical compliance with Israeli law.
Source: ICLG - Israel Gambling Laws 2026 • Chambers - Gaming Law Israel • Herzog Law

Likely Interview Questions

REGULATORY KNOWLEDGE
How would you structure Plaee’s operations to comply with CFTC event contract regulations while also managing the sweepstakes business across multiple US states?
Framework: Explain the dual regulatory regime: CFTC for prediction markets (federal, exclusive jurisdiction per KalshiEx v. CFTC) vs. state-by-state sweepstakes regulation. Reference the Third Circuit ruling (April 2026) supporting federal pre-emption for event contracts. For sweepstakes, discuss the three-element gambling test (consideration, chance, prize) and how the free-entry mechanism removes consideration. Acknowledge the state ban trend (6 states in 2025) and discuss a compliance matrix approach to track which states remain viable. Mention that Plaee’s Crypto.com partnership already provides the CFTC-regulated exchange infrastructure.
COMPLIANCE
What AML/KYC framework would you implement for our B2C gaming operations?
Key points: Discuss risk-based approach — tiered KYC (simplified for low-value, enhanced for high-value players), transaction monitoring with automated suspicious activity detection, SAR filing procedures, PEP screening, and beneficial ownership verification. Reference the CFTC’s insider trading advisory (Feb 2026) and the need for market manipulation detection. For inabit’s payment layer, discuss how self-custodial architecture affects AML obligations — even though users control their own keys, the platform still needs to comply with AML as a regulated entity. Mention SOC 2 Type II as a foundation for compliance controls.
CORPORATE STRUCTURE
How would you advise on structuring the Plaee group across jurisdictions — Israel, Cyprus, and the US?
Key points: Discuss the common Israel/Cyprus structure in gaming tech: R&D and IP in Israel, regulated gaming entity in Cyprus (MGA license potential), US-facing operations through the Crypto.com DCM partnership (TSP/IB/FCM structure). Address transfer pricing between entities, substance requirements (real employees/decision-making in each jurisdiction), and the need to avoid piercing the corporate veil. Reference your experience with multi-jurisdictional structures at Odsy Foundation (Switzerland/global). Mention Plaee’s press release listing Limassol, Cyprus as their contact address.
STRATEGIC
What are the biggest legal risks facing Plaee in the next 12 months?
Top risks to discuss: (1) US state-level challenges to prediction markets (19 federal lawsuits pending; Massachusetts injunction vs. Third Circuit pre-emption creates circuit split). (2) Sweepstakes state bans accelerating (Florida HB 591 could add felony penalties). (3) CFTC rulemaking uncertainty — the ANPRM is requesting comments, and final rules could impose new requirements. (4) DeFi regulatory risk for the global track — international bans on prediction platforms (Polymarket banned in 33+ countries). (5) Insider trading enforcement creating liability exposure. Show you understand the landscape but frame each risk with mitigation strategies.
EXPERIENCE
Tell us about your experience with crypto/blockchain legal work. How does it prepare you for a gaming/prediction market role?
Bridge your crypto experience to gaming: At dWallet Labs, you dealt with regulatory classification of digital assets, compliance frameworks, and cross-jurisdictional licensing — the same core skills needed for prediction markets. Odsy Foundation (Switzerland) exposed you to self-custodial custody models — directly relevant to inabit’s TCM technology and Plaee’s DeFi track. Emphasize that prediction markets are built on crypto/DeFi infrastructure, so your technical legal understanding is a significant advantage. Acknowledge the gaming-specific knowledge gap (sweepstakes law, gambling licensing) and frame it as a targeted learning curve on a strong existing foundation.
PRACTICAL
How would you build the legal function at Plaee from scratch?
90-day plan approach: Month 1: Audit all existing contracts, corporate entities, regulatory filings, and compliance gaps across the group. Map every jurisdiction where Plaee operates or plans to operate. Month 2: Establish core legal processes — contract templates (B2B operator agreements, B2C terms of service), compliance policies (AML/KYC, responsible gambling), IP protection strategy. Month 3: Build external counsel network across key jurisdictions (US gaming attorneys, Cyprus/MGA specialists, Israel corporate), implement contract management system, create board governance framework. Ongoing: Regulatory tracking dashboard for CFTC rulemaking, state sweepstakes laws, and international prediction market regulations.
TECHNICAL
How do you view the legal implications of self-custodial architecture vs. custodial models in the context of our platform?
Leverage your inabit/DeFi knowledge: Self-custodial architecture (inabit’s TCM model) means the platform never takes custody of user funds — this has profound regulatory implications. It may reduce certain obligations under money transmission laws (since you’re not holding funds), but doesn’t eliminate AML/KYC requirements for the platform itself. Reference the FinCEN guidance on convertible virtual currency and unhosted wallets. For Plaee’s DeFi track, discuss how smart contract-based settlement shifts risk — the platform provides the infrastructure but the execution is on-chain. Frame this as both a legal advantage (limited custody liability) and a challenge (ensuring compliance when you don’t control the assets).
COMMERCIAL
Walk us through how you’d structure a B2B agreement with a new prediction market operator wanting to use Plaee’s platform.
Key commercial terms: Platform licensing fee structure (SaaS model, revenue share, or hybrid); operator’s regulatory pathway (TSP, IB, or FCM — each has different obligations); data ownership and usage rights; liability allocation for user-facing compliance; IP licensing terms for white-label UI; SLA commitments (uptime, settlement speed); jurisdiction-specific addenda for markets the operator will serve; termination provisions accounting for regulatory changes; indemnification for regulatory actions. Show awareness of both the commercial and regulatory dimensions of each deal.
